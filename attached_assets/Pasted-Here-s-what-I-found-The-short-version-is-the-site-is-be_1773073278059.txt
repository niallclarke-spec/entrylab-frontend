Here's what I found. The short version is: the site is being indexed by Google but there's a significant content rendering problem.
What's discoverable via Google:
A site:entrylab.io search shows several pages are indexed, including:

/broker-guides/hero10x-review ✅
/broker/gatesfx-review ✅
/broker-news/ospreyfx-closes-its-doors... ✅
/kot4x-shuts-down-what-you-should-know/ ✅ (also at /article/kot4x-...)
/contact/, /what-we-do/, homepage ✅

So Google is finding and indexing the URLs — that part is working.
The problem — content isn't rendering for crawlers or AI:
When I fetched the homepage, the Hero10X page, and other pages, they all returned almost nothing — just the page <title> tag. No body content, no article text, no headings, no structured data. This tells me the site is almost certainly built as a client-side JavaScript app (React/Next.js/similar), and:

Crawlers and AI search tools are getting back a near-empty HTML shell
The actual content only loads after JS executes in a browser
Google can sometimes render JS (via headless Chrome), which is why URLs are indexed — but snippets in search results are thin (you can see from the search results: the Hero10X page snippet is just one line)
AI tools (like me, Perplexity, ChatGPT) that fetch pages to answer questions would get essentially nothing from your content pages

What to check/fix:

Server-Side Rendering (SSR) or Static Generation — if you're on Next.js, ensure pages are using getStaticProps or getServerSideProps, not purely client-side rendering. If it's a headless WordPress or similar, check the front-end framework config.
robots.txt — I couldn't directly fetch it due to tool restrictions, but you should verify it's not accidentally blocking crawlers. Check entrylab.io/robots.txt in your browser.
Meta tags / AI crawlers — some sites now block AI scrapers (GPTBot, ClaudeBot, PerplexityBot) via robots.txt. Worth checking if that's the case.
Structured data — no schema markup appeared in the fetched content (e.g. Article, Review, BreadcrumbList). Adding JSON-LD would significantly help both Google rich results and AI discoverability.

The core issue is the JS rendering gap. Your content exists, Google knows the URLs exist, but neither Google nor AI tools are reading the actual article body — which is a meaningful SEO and AI visibility problem for a content-heavy site like Entrylab.
